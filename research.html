Government Affiliation and Rating Inequality: Uncovering the Corporate Credit Rating "Black Box"
with Lei Ge (Renmin University of China)  ·  Working Paper, 2025
This paper investigates how government customer relationships generate persistent inequality in corporate credit ratings and how this inequality evolves across macroeconomic crises. Using a panel of 132,348 US firm-year observations from 2000 to 2025, we combine shock-interacted difference-in-differences designs with Debiased Machine Learning (DML) and Causal Forest estimators. We find four results. First, government affiliation confers a genuine causal rating premium that amplifies sharply in the post-GFC decade and partially reverts during COVID-19, while remaining significant at the investment-grade boundary throughout. Second, this premium operates at the extensive margin: rating agencies reward the existence of a government relationship rather than its revenue magnitude. Third, the premium accrues almost exclusively to investment-grade firms; for speculative-grade firms, government affiliation provides no buffering post-GFC and reverses into a significant rating penalty during the pandemic. Fourth, within the investment-grade group, the premium concentrates among low-leverage, high-margin firms in sectors where government procurement is rare but credible. Together, these findings demonstrate that the rating ''black box'' encodes a time-varying, crisis-contingent signal that systematically widens---rather than narrows---the creditworthiness gap between firms on opposite sides of the investment-grade boundary.
